To achieve financial security, how much you save is always more important than the amount you earn or how shrewdly you invest. If you are under 30 years old, your goal should be to save 20% of your monthly income after tax deductions. This is irrespective of how much you earn. Approximately 50% should be reserved for the essentials like food and accommodation. The remaining 30% is for recreation and entertainment. But for many young people, it’ll be difficult to designate such a large proportion of their income for savings.

If you find it hard to save any money at all, start by cutting all unnecessary spending, allocate a tiny amount of 1 or 2 percent for savings, and gradually increase that amount. Always keep that 20% goal in mind to prevent yourself from becoming complacent.

It can be challenging to stick to such a strict plan. But if you adopt the right mindset, you should be able to make it work for you.

So, what should you be doing with the money that you’re saving? Some must be kept easily accessible in case you need some cash in an emergency. The largest proportion should be invested in retirement plans, either through your employer or privately. And you can keep some money for high-risk but potentially lucrative investments. Dividends can be reinvested or used to purchase something you like. By following this plan, you should hopefully be able to enjoy your life now, and still be financially secure in the future.

#### Recording 1

Did you know that Americans have approximately three times the amount of space we had 50 years ago? Therefore, you'd think we'd have sufficient room for all of our possessions. On the contrary, the personal storage business is now a growing industry. We've got triple the space, but we've become such enthusiastic consumers that we require even more. This phenomenon has resulted in significant credit card debt, enormous environmental footprints, and perhaps not coincidentally, our happiness levels have failed to increase over the same half century.

I'm not saying that we should all live in tinier apartments, but consider the benefits of an edited life. When you return home and walk through your front door, take a moment to ask yourselves: "Could I do with a little life editing? Will that give me more freedom and more time?"

#### Recording 2

Now, believe it or not, people sometimes lie in order to maintain a good, honest reputation – even if it hurts them to do so. At least, this is what a team of scientists is suggesting, with evidence to prove it.

Picture this scenario: You often drive for work and can be compensated for up to 400 miles per month. Most people at your company drive about 300 miles each month. But this month, you drove 400 miles. How many miles do you think you'd claim in your expense report?

The scientists asked this exact question as part of the study we’re discussing today. With surprising results, they found that 12% of respondents reported the distance they drove as less than the actual figure, giving an average answer of 384 miles. In other words, they lied about the number of miles, even though they would forfeit money they were owed.

The researchers believe this was to seem honest, with the assumption being that others would be suspicious of a high expense claim. But why would people fabricate numbers to their own detriment? The researchers explained that many people care a great deal about their reputation and how they’ll be judged by others. If they care enough, their concern about appearing honest and not losing the respect of others may be greater than their desire to actually be honest.

The researchers assert that their findings suggest that when people obtain very favorable outcomes, they anticipate other people's suspicious reactions and prefer lying and appearing honest to telling the truth and appearing as selfish liars.

So why is this research important? Well, experts generally agree there are two main types of lie: selfish lies and lies that are meant to benefit others. The first, as you may predict, is for selfish gain, such as submitting a fraudulent claim to an insurance company, while the second involves lying to help others or not offend others, for example, telling a friend whose outfit you don’t like that they look great.

But the researchers are suggesting a third type of lying: lying to maintain a good reputation. Now this hypothesis is new and some skeptics argue that this isn’t a whole new category of lie.

The findings seem intuitive to me. After all, one of the main motivations for lying is to increase our worth in the eyes of others, so it seems highly likely that people will lie to seem honest.

#### Recording 3

Why do old people dislike new music? As I’ve grown older, I often hear people my age say things like "they just don’t make good music like they used to." Why does this happen? Luckily, psychology can give us some insights into this puzzle. Musical tastes begin to crystallize as early as age 13 or 14. By the time we’re in our early 20s, these tastes get locked into place pretty firmly.

In fact, studies have found that by the time we turn 33, most of us have stopped listening to new music. Meanwhile, popular songs released when you’re in your early teens are likely to remain quite popular among your age group for the rest of your life.

There could be a biological explanation for this, as there’s evidence that the brain’s ability to make subtle distinctions between different chords, rhythms, and melodies deteriorates with age. So to older people, newer, less familiar songs might all “sound the same.”

But there are maybe some simpler reasons for older people’s aversion to newer music. One of the most researched laws of social psychology is something called the “mere exposure effect,” which in essence means that the more we’re exposed to something, the more we tend to like it.

This happens with people we know, the advertisements we see, and the songs we listen to. When you’re in your early teens, you probably spend a fair amount of time listening to music or watching music videos. Your favorite songs and artists become familiar, comforting parts of your routine.

For many people over 30, job and family obligations increase, so there’s less time to spend discovering new music. Instead, many will simply listen to old, familiar favorites from that period of their lives when they had more free time.

Of course, those teen years weren’t necessarily carefree. They’re famously confusing, which is why so many TV shows and movies revolve around high school turmoil. Psychology research has shown that the emotions that we experience as teens seem more intense than those that come later. And we also know that intense emotions are associated with stronger memories and preferences. Both of these might explain why the songs we listen to during this period become so memorable and beloved.

So there’s nothing wrong with your parents because they don’t like your music. Rather it’s all part of the natural order of things.

#### Recording 1

For many years, researchers have studied how emotions affect our well-being. But until recently, most of this research has focused on positive emotions versus negative emotions. This simple classification, however, overlooks the fact that emotions vary in intensity as well as in valence.

A new study conducted by psychologists at the University of California, Berkeley, has found that solitude has a different effect on high-arousal emotions than on low-arousal emotions. High-arousal emotions include things like anger, fear, excitement and joy. Low-arousal emotions include sadness, contentment, calmness and boredom.

In the study, participants were asked to sit alone in a room without any distractions for 15 minutes. Before and after this period, they completed a questionnaire measuring their emotional states. The researchers found that solitude had a reductive effect on high-arousal emotions, both positive and negative. That is, people felt less angry, less fearful, less excited and less joyful after sitting alone for 15 minutes.

However, solitude had no significant effect on low-arousal emotions. This suggests that solitude may be particularly effective at calming us down when we are feeling highly aroused, whether positively or negatively. It also challenges the common belief that solitude is always a negative experience that makes people feel sad or lonely.

The researchers note that these findings have important implications for our daily lives. For example, if you’re feeling stressed or angry after a long day at work, taking a few minutes to sit alone in a quiet room may help you calm down. Similarly, if you’re feeling overly excited and need to focus, solitude can help you center yourself.

#### Recording 2

Did you know that your brain uses more energy than any other organ in your body? Even though it makes up only about 2% of your body weight, it consumes approximately 20% of your total daily energy expenditure. This high energy consumption is due to the fact that your brain is constantly active, even when you’re sleeping.

Most of this energy is used to power the electrical signals that neurons use to communicate with each other. These signals are responsible for everything from your thoughts and memories to your movements and senses. In fact, even when you’re resting, your brain is still working hard to maintain basic functions like breathing, heart rate and body temperature.

Contrary to popular belief, the brain does not use significantly more energy when you’re engaged in difficult cognitive tasks like solving math problems or learning a new language. Studies have shown that the difference in energy consumption between resting and performing complex mental tasks is only about 5%. This is because most of the brain’s energy is used for ongoing, background processes that are essential for survival.

So where does all this energy come from? The brain relies almost exclusively on glucose for fuel. Glucose is a type of sugar that is obtained from the food we eat. It is transported to the brain through the bloodstream, where it is used to produce ATP, the molecule that provides energy for all cellular processes.

Because the brain cannot store glucose, it needs a constant supply of it from the blood. If blood glucose levels drop too low, the brain cannot function properly, which can lead to symptoms like dizziness, confusion and even loss of consciousness. This is why it’s important to eat a balanced diet that provides a steady supply of glucose throughout the day.

#### Recording 3

One of the most common questions asked in a job interview is "Tell me about yourself." This question may seem simple, but it is actually one of the most important questions you will be asked. It gives you the opportunity to make a good first impression and to highlight your skills and experience.

Many job seekers make the mistake of answering this question by reciting their resume. This is a big mistake because the interviewer has already read your resume. What they want to know is who you are as a person, what your strengths are, and why you are the best candidate for the job.

The best way to answer "Tell me about yourself" is to tell a story. Your story should be concise, engaging, and relevant to the job you are applying for. It should highlight your most important skills and experiences, and it should show how you have used those skills to achieve success in the past.

When telling your story, it’s important to focus on your achievements rather than your responsibilities. For example, instead of saying "I was responsible for managing a team of five people," you could say "I led a team of five people to increase sales by 25% in one year." This shows the interviewer that you are results-oriented and that you can deliver tangible results.

It’s also important to tailor your story to the specific job you are applying for. Before the interview, take the time to research the company and the job description. Identify the key skills and qualities that the company is looking for, and make sure your story highlights those skills and qualities.

Finally, practice your story before the interview. The more you practice, the more confident and natural you will sound. Remember, the goal of answering "Tell me about yourself" is to make the interviewer want to learn more about you. If you can do that, you will be well on your way to getting the job.
