Is there any way to get better at detecting lies? The new research offers some surprising advice. Stop looking and listen instead. It turns out that if we're unable to see the face, but rather focus on the voice of the person in question, our accuracy rate improves considerably.

In other words, your team had vitality. Vitality comes about when the ties people form with their fellow team members are such that they stay connected even after the team breaks up. What characteristics of a team make its members more likely to stay in contact despite no longer working together? This question has been answered recently in a study published in a business journal. One of the two key factors the research team discovered is sameness, specifically sharing the same gender or ethnic origin. The more members of a team share similar demographics, the more inclined they'll be to remain associates long after the team has served its purpose. After ties are established, similarity strengthens them. As a result, they regard these individuals with greater trust and mutual understanding, which motivates them to seek further opportunities for collaboration. In effect, the more people in a team have in common, the stronger their loyalty to each other and the team will be. The second factor is that the experience must be memorable. Teams that have been through intense experiences together, such as working on a difficult project or dealing with a crisis, are more likely to stay in touch. These shared experiences create a bond that is hard to break. The study also found that the size of the team matters. Smaller teams are more likely to have strong ties than larger teams. Skin may seem like a superficial human attribute, but it is the first thing we notice about anyone we meet. As a zoologist focusing on the studies of apes and monkeys, I've been studying why humans evolved to become the naked ape, and why skin comes in so many different shades around the world. We can make a very good estimate from the fossil record that humans probably evolved naked skin around a million and a half years ago. And meanwhile, they mostly lost their coat of fur. Today, we have only a few patches of hair left on our bodies. Why did we lose our fur? Well, one theory is that it helped us to stay cool when we started walking upright and moving long distances in the hot African sun. Another theory is that it helped us to avoid parasites such as ticks and lice. But the most interesting theory is that it helped us to communicate with each other. Our skin is full of nerve endings that allow us to feel touch, pressure, and temperature. By losing our fur, we were able to use touch as a form of communication, such as hugging, kissing, and holding hands. As for the different shades of skin color, that is due to the amount of melanin in the skin. Melanin is a pigment that protects the skin from the harmful effects of the sun's ultraviolet rays. People who live near the equator have more melanin in their skin, which makes it darker. People who live farther from the equator have less melanin, which makes their skin lighter. This is because in areas with less sunlight, the body needs to produce more vitamin D, which is made when the skin is exposed to sunlight. Having lighter skin allows more sunlight to penetrate the skin, which helps the body to produce more vitamin D.
