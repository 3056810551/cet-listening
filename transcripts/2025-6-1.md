When a person tries to access a memory, their brains quickly examine everything stored in it to find the relevant information. But as we age, many of us have difficulty retrieving memories. In a review published in the journal _Trends in Cognitive Sciences_ on February 11, researchers propose an explanation for why this might be happening: the brains of older adults allocate more space to accumulated knowledge and have more material to navigate when attempting to access memories. While this wealth of prior knowledge can make memory retrieval challenging, the researchers say it has its upsides—this life experience can aid with creativity and decision-making.

Researchers Tarek Amer, Jordana Wynn and Lynn Hasher looked at several behavioral and brain-imaging studies, which show that older adults have difficulty suppressing information that is no longer relevant and that when searching for a specific memory, they often retrieve other, irrelevant memories along with it. The studies also showed that when given a cognitive task, older adults rely more heavily on previous knowledge than younger adults do.

While the researchers focus primarily on the difficulties that these disorderly-crowded memories may pose, they also highlight a few situations in which these same memory patterns may be useful. Evidence suggests that older adults show preserved, and at times enhanced, creativity as a function of enriched memories, the researchers write. They further hypothesize that older adults may be well served by their prior knowledge when it comes to decision-making, where they can pull on their accumulated wisdom.

## PASSAGE 2

There are at least four major problems with work in America today. First, work can be alienating. Workers are often not in control of how they work, when they work, what is done with the goods and services they produce, and what is done with the profits made from their work. Second, workers are not paid the full value of their labor. Real wages have not kept pace with productivity, driving economic inequality and a decline in labor's share of income. Third, people are time poor. In the U.S., full-time employees work an average of 8.72 hours per day despite productivity increases. Long working hours contribute to the feeling of time poverty, which has an adverse effect on psychological well-being. Fourth, automation puts jobs and wages at risk. Technological innovation could in theory liberate people from the 40-hour workweek, but unless changes are made to the structure of work, automation will simply exert downward pressure on wages and further diminish work opportunities.

So, what can be done to alleviate these problems? There are competing visions of the best way to arrive at a solution. One vision is what's known as the post-work position. The post-work theorists believe that work should not hold a central position in our lives or take up so much of our time. They assert that, although doing some work might be necessary, meaning, purpose and social value reside in the communities and relationships built and sustained outside of the workplace. Thus, life should be seen as an end in itself.

Recent studies show that self-control and persistence are important predictors of academic success. But how do children learn these skills? Previous research has shown that children can learn persistence from adults. For example, if children see an adult work hard to achieve a goal, they are more likely to persist in their own difficult tasks. But what if children only see the end result of an adult's hard work, not the process?

To explore this question, researchers studied 15-month-old babies. In one experiment, the babies watched an adult struggle to open a container for 30 seconds before succeeding. In another experiment, the babies watched the adult open the container easily on the first try. Then, the babies were given a toy that played music when they pressed a hidden button. The researchers found that the babies who had watched the adult struggle pressed the button more times and for longer periods of time than the babies who had watched the adult succeed easily.

The results suggest that babies can learn persistence from watching adults struggle. This is important because it shows that children don't need to be explicitly taught persistence. They can learn it simply by observing the behavior of adults around them. The researchers also found that the babies were more likely to persist if the adult made eye contact with them and talked to them while struggling. This suggests that social interaction plays an important role in learning persistence.

If chronic lateness is a problem for you, you're not alone. Many people struggle with being on time, and it can have negative consequences for their careers, relationships and self-esteem. But there are things you can do to overcome habitual lateness.

First, if mornings are a struggle for you, do as much as you can to ready yourself the night before. Lay out your clothes, pack your bag, and prepare your lunch. This will save you time in the morning and reduce the stress of rushing around. Second, learn to leave time in between tasks and meetings. It's easy to overschedule yourself, but this often leads to lateness. If you have back-to-back meetings, you won't have time to get from one place to another, and you'll end up being late. Third, calculate transit time between activities and then add 10 to 30 minutes for unexpected delays. Traffic, public transportation problems, and other unforeseen events can all make you late, so it's important to build in a buffer.

Finally, be honest with yourself about why you're late. Are you trying to do too much? Do you underestimate how long tasks take? Once you identify the root cause of your lateness, you can take steps to address it. Remember, being on time is a habit, and like any habit, it takes time and effort to change. But with practice, you can become a more punctual person.
