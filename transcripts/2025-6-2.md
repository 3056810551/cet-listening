Think your refrigerator is just one big box of evenly cold air? Not so! The temperature inside the average fridge actually varies from front to back, bottom to top and everywhere in between. Because of this, food product placement within the appliance is far more important than you probably realize. The whole purpose of the modern refrigerator is to slow the growth of bacteria, which thrive in temperatures from 4.4 to 60 °C. As a result, refrigerators are typically set at or below that low end to protect foods against bacteria. Although it might seem like time spent organizing the fridge will spoil your day, doing so will actually make it more user-friendly, easier to clean and food safety-conscious.

Those little bins on the inside of refrigerator doors are convenient, but they don't maintain a consistent temperature, so definitely refrain from putting perishable foods in there. The top area is not as cold as the bottom of the fridge, so save it for the foods that don't need extra-chilly temperatures. Store foods that are ready-to-eat or already cooked on the top shelf. The middle shelf is the most temperature-controlled part of the fridge, so place eggs here. Drinks and prepared foods can also live on the middle shelf if you run out of room up top. The back of the bottom shelf is the coldest spot in the fridge, so store perishable dairy products here to keep them edible longer. Never store foods with high water content here, because the water within them can and will freeze, effectively ruining them.

Cooking schools are great for teaching you how to make a delicious meal, but they don't teach you basic organization. And that's a problem, because being organized is essential for being a good cook. When you're organized, you can work more efficiently, avoid mistakes, and enjoy the cooking process more.

For professional chefs, organization is a way of life. They call it "mise en place", which means "everything in its place". Before they start cooking, they prepare all of their ingredients, measure them out, and arrange them in order of use. This allows them to work quickly and smoothly, without having to stop and look for things.

Home cooks can benefit from adopting the same approach. By taking a few minutes to organize your ingredients and tools before you start cooking, you can save a lot of time and frustration. You'll also be less likely to forget an ingredient or make a mistake.

In addition to organization, cooking also teaches you how to use time and resources wisely. When you're cooking, you have to plan ahead, prioritize tasks, and make the most of what you have. These are valuable skills that can be applied to other areas of your life.

So, if you want to become a better cook, don't just focus on learning recipes and techniques. Take the time to learn how to be organized and how to use your time and resources wisely. These skills will not only make you a better cook, but they will also make you a more productive and successful person in general.

Goals are often seen as the basis for self-improvement. We set goals to lose weight, get a promotion, learn a new skill, and so on. But what if goals are actually holding us back? What if the very act of setting goals is preventing us from achieving the things we want?

The problem with goals is that they are often too rigid. We set a goal to lose 10 pounds in three months, and if we don't achieve it, we feel like failures. But life is unpredictable, and things don't always go according to plan. We might get sick, have a busy week at work, or simply lose motivation. When this happens, our goals become a source of stress and frustration, rather than a source of motivation.

Another problem with goals is that they can make us stop imagining further progress in life. Once we achieve a goal, we often feel like we've "made it" and we stop trying to improve. But self-improvement is a lifelong journey, not a destination. We should always be looking for ways to grow and develop, even after we've achieved our goals.

So, what's the alternative? Instead of focusing on goals, we should focus on progress. Instead of measuring ourselves against a fixed goal, we should measure where we've come from. This allows us to celebrate our achievements, no matter how small, and it keeps us motivated to continue improving. It also allows us to adapt to changes in our lives and adjust our expectations accordingly.
