W: But they aren't sacrifices. When people change their values, their desires change. So, in the example of housing, if we value relationships, sharing a home isn't depriving ourselves of space but giving us an opportunity to spend more time with our loved ones.
M: Indeed it is.

Forgiveness is the release of resentment or anger. Forgiveness doesn't mean reconciliation. We don't have to return to the same relationship, nor do we have to accept the same harmful behaviors from an offender.

Forgiveness is vitally important for the mental health of certain victims. It propels people forward rather than keeping them emotionally engaged in an injustice or trauma. Carrying the hurt or anger of an offense leads the body to release stress chemicals. Eliminating the perpetual flow of those chemicals may also explain why forgiveness provides physical health benefits.

There are scenarios in which forgiveness is not the best course. Sometimes the victim becomes more empowered when they give themselves permission not to forgive.

Forgiveness can be challenging. This is especially true when the offending party offers an insincere apology, or maybe they haven't offered anything at all. However, it's often the healthiest path forward.

It's important to cultivate forgiveness by developing compassion for the offender, reflect on whether the act was due to malicious intent or whether it was caused by challenging circumstances in the offender's life.

What about forgiving ourselves?

We sometimes need to take responsibility for mistakes, but intense guilt and shame aren't a desirable outcome in the long run. Forgiving yourself may seem like an ambiguous process. You can begin by acknowledging that you are at fault. Take responsibility for the hurt you caused, then reflect on why the event occurred. Draw the lessons you learned and try to avoid committing a similar offense in the future.

In its long history, it has never been expanded, remaining as a single loop line with a mere 15 stations. At its peak, it served the shipyard workers on the south side of the city.

It ran with little further change until 1977 when its new operators closed it for major modernization investment. The subway in its present form reopened for operation in April 1980. Since its relaunch, the subway has seen a revival in its fortunes. It serves as a viable alternative to other forms of transport and has gone a long way to alleviate traffic jams in the city centre.

In 1996, the system reached an important milestone—100 years. To commemorate this special event, the colour scheme of the train carriages was updated.

The smartcard provides more convenient travel, and passengers simply top up their cards and tap them to get in and out the subway. Why do we form opinions or attitudes about someone or something without really knowing much about them? Just hearing something good or bad about a person, a place, or a thing can influence our opinions without us even realizing it.

Forming opinions about someone or something before really knowing them well is called prejudice. Prejudice means "pre-judging" something. It means making a judgment about something before you have all the facts.

Prejudiced attitudes are usually based on myths, half-truths, or incorrect information. This is dangerous because they can prevent us from getting to know the truth about someone or something. They can also lead to discrimination, which is treating people unfairly because of their race, religion, gender, or other characteristics.

Prejudice can be found in all societies, and it can affect people of all ages. It can be passed down from generation to generation, and it can be reinforced by the media and by our peers.

So when do we begin to develop prejudiced attitudes? Well, research shows that children as young as three years old can start to form prejudices. This is because they are influenced by the attitudes of their parents and other adults around them.

As we grow older, our prejudices can become more deeply ingrained. We may start to believe that our own group or culture is superior to others. This is called ethnocentrism. When pride in our own group turns into a feeling of superiority, we begin to develop prejudiced attitudes that can be harmful to others.
